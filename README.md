# Business decision bias library

A practical reference of 55 cognitive biases that distort business decisions – budgets, forecasts, hiring, pricing, campaigns, vendor selection, and strategy. Each bias gets one card in a standard format, with examples drawn from marketing team scenarios.

This is not an academic catalog. The filter for inclusion: the bias must plausibly cause a real business decision to go wrong, and there must be a concrete countermeasure worth applying.

## How to use this library

- **Before a significant decision:** scan the category most relevant to the decision type (forecasting a launch → category A; killing or continuing a program → category C; group review meeting → category D).
- **In postmortems:** use the cards as a checklist to identify which biases contributed to a bad call, then adopt the countermeasures as process changes.
- **For training:** each card stands alone. Use one card per week in team meetings, discuss the marketing example, and ask where the team last saw the pattern.

## Card format

Every card follows the same structure:

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Definition | What the bias is, in 1-2 plain sentences |
| Impact on decisions | How it distorts business choices, and the mechanism |
| Detection signals | Phrases and behaviors that indicate the bias is active |
| Countermeasures | Concrete practices to prevent the wrong decision |
| Marketing example | A marketing team making a specific bad call because of this bias |
| Related biases | Cross-references to other cards |

## Scope notes

- Mirror-image pairs are merged into single cards: horn effect into halo effect (34), default effect into status quo bias (24).
- Persuasion principles (reciprocity, commitment/consistency, social proof as an influence tactic) are excluded: they describe how others influence you by design, not how your own judgment fails. Their defensive counterparts appear where relevant (bandwagon, scarcity, decoy).
- Marketing examples use realistic but fictional figures. They illustrate the mechanism, not benchmarks.

## Grounding

The catalog draws on established decision science: Kahneman and Tversky's heuristics and biases program, Thaler's behavioral economics, and standard organizational-behavior research on group decision-making. Taxonomy boundaries are judgment calls; several biases overlap by design, and the Related biases section on each card maps those overlaps.
