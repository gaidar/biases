# 54 – Decoy effect

**Category:** F – Action and decision structure

## Definition

Adding a third, deliberately inferior option shifts preference between the original two. Marketers use it in pricing pages; buyers of marketing services and software fall for it in vendor quotes.

## Impact on decisions

Purchases anchor on constructed comparisons instead of actual needs. The "obvious value" option in a vendor's pricing is usually the one the vendor designed you to choose.

## Detection signals

- A middle option that becomes attractive only in contrast to a slightly worse, similarly priced one
- Evaluation focused on comparing tiers rather than mapping tiers to requirements
- "For just X more you get..." driving the decision

## Countermeasures

- Map your actual requirements to features before looking at pricing structure
- Evaluate each option on absolute value against needs, ignoring the others
- Remove the least attractive option mentally and check whether your preference changes

## Marketing example

A martech vendor quotes Basic at $20K, Pro at $45K, and Enterprise at $50K. Enterprise looks like obvious value next to Pro – and the team signs it. Requirements mapping done afterward shows Pro covered every actual need. The $5K gap was engineered to make a $50K sale feel like a bargain; the extra features go unused.
